60 Oxford Street presented a project controls challenge that few programs do: three fundamentally different program types — data center infrastructure, active research labs, and academic classroom environments — built out simultaneously within a single structure on a dense urban campus. Each environment has its own commissioning requirements, schedule logic, and end-user expectations. Keeping them coordinated under a single controls framework was the core of the work.

## Facility Overview

The 60 Oxford Street building serves Harvard's Faculty of Arts and Sciences and houses a mix of server rooms and data center space, faculty research laboratories, and teaching and classroom facilities. The building had to be delivered to multiple user groups with different definitions of readiness — data center operations, research activation, and academic scheduling — all of which had to align.

## Role — Owner's Representative, Project Controls

I represented the owner on project controls, embedded in the delivery process to maintain schedule and cost discipline across all three program elements.

Data center spaces have the most demanding commissioning requirements: power systems, cooling infrastructure, redundancy testing, and IT readiness checks all have to happen in sequence before a server room is live. Any slip in that sequence affects go-live. I tracked those dependencies closely and flagged compression risks before they became problems.

Research lab environments brought a different kind of complexity: end-user involvement. Faculty and researchers have specific requirements for their spaces that often evolve during construction. Managing scope changes, tracking their impact, and keeping the cost and schedule baseline defensible required consistent documentation and active communication.

Classroom environments are the most schedule-constrained: they have to be ready for a semester start date. That deadline is immovable, and every delay in adjacent scopes creates pressure on the academic fit-out. I maintained a separate schedule watch on those areas to ensure they were protected throughout the delivery.

## Coordination Across Program Types

The real controls challenge on a project like this is that the three program types don't share schedule logic or commissioning requirements. Managing them as separate tracks within a unified program — sharing space, shared contractors, and a single integrated schedule — required constant attention to interface points and a willingness to escalate when coordination broke down.

## Outcome

All three program elements were delivered and activated. The data center reached operational readiness, research labs were commissioned for occupancy, and classroom spaces were ready for academic use on schedule.
