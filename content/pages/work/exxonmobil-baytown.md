The ExxonMobil Baytown complex in Baytown, Texas is one of the largest integrated industrial facilities in the world — a combined refinery and petrochemical complex operating across hundreds of process units with continuous operation. The Baytown Expansion program was a multi-billion-dollar capital investment to expand and upgrade production capacity, executed under Fluor Enterprises as the engineering, procurement, and construction contractor.

## Program Scale

The program operated at a scale that most capital programs never approach. At peak, thousands of craft workers were on site across multiple concurrent scopes — refinery expansion, new petrochemical units, utilities upgrades, and supporting infrastructure. The engineering scope alone involved hundreds of engineers across multiple Fluor offices coordinating deliverables across disciplines and geographies.

On a program this size, project controls is not a support function. It's the operational backbone. Without a functioning controls framework — baseline schedule, cost tracking, EVM, change management, reporting — the program cannot be managed. It can only be reacted to.

## Role — Project Controls, Fluor Enterprises

I worked on project controls within the Fluor delivery team, contributing to schedule development, baseline management, progress measurement, and controls reporting across assigned scopes.

Schedule work at this scale involves multi-level schedule integration: engineering schedules, procurement schedules, construction schedules, and commissioning schedules all have to align into a coherent program logic. Changes at any level cascade, and maintaining baseline integrity while absorbing scope changes requires a disciplined change management process.

Earned value reporting connected physical progress to cost performance, giving the owner and project leadership a quantitative view of program health that went beyond activity completion rates. On a program measured in billions, a 1% variance is tens of millions of dollars — controlling that exposure requires early identification, not post-mortem analysis.

## Working at Industrial Scale

The Baytown program shaped my understanding of what project controls actually requires at the top tier of capital delivery. The tools are the same — schedules, cost accounts, EVA — but the discipline, the rigor, and the consequences of a missed signal are categorically different. You develop an instinct for where the risks are accumulating before the numbers confirm it, and you build the reporting structures that let leadership see what they need to see, when they need to see it.

This experience, combined with similar programs on Dow LHC-9, BP Whiting Refinery, and Chevron Phillips Hydrocracker, defined the controls standard I've carried through every program since.
