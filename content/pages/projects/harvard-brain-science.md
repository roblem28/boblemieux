The Harvard Center for Brain Science occupies space within the Northwest Science Building in Cambridge — a large-scale interdisciplinary research facility built to consolidate neuroscience, imaging, and computational research under one roof. The program was designed to be flexible by nature: research needs evolve, equipment changes, and the building had to support long-term adaptability across a high-demand academic environment.

## The Facility

The Northwest Science Building encompasses approximately 530,000 SF and houses multiple scientific programs simultaneously. Lab environments range from standard bench science to high-sensitivity imaging suites and computational research spaces. The building's mechanical, electrical, and control systems are built to a level of performance that most commercial construction never approaches — vibration thresholds, air quality standards, and power conditioning requirements that are driven by the instruments, not the occupants.

Operating within that environment means the project controls framework has to account for more than schedule and budget. Equipment procurement, commissioning sequencing, and end-user readiness are all interdependent in ways that don't exist on a standard commercial build.

## Role — Owner's Representative, Project Controls

I represented the owner's interests on project controls, working alongside design and construction teams to maintain structure and clarity across a technically demanding and stakeholder-heavy program.

Specific responsibilities included schedule tracking and alignment across multiple concurrent work fronts, cost control and forecasting through active change management, and coordination between the design team, general contractor, specialty subcontractors, and Harvard's end-user groups — the researchers, facilities staff, and faculty stakeholders with direct input on lab configurations and technical requirements.

A significant part of the role was managing the gap between what researchers wanted and what the construction schedule could accommodate. Research programs don't pause for construction logic. Keeping those two realities in alignment — without losing cost control or scope clarity — required consistent communication and a controls framework that could flex without losing discipline.

## Complexity Factors

This was not a standard building project. The complexity came from several intersecting sources:

The mechanical and electrical systems were specified to support research-grade environments. That level of precision in installation and commissioning creates schedule dependencies that cascade across the job — one delayed commissioning activity affects occupancy readiness for an entire lab cluster.

Specialized research equipment often arrives on lead times that don't respond to construction schedules. Procurement tracking and installation sequencing had to be managed proactively, not reactively.

Program changes were frequent and often legitimate — research priorities shift, new equipment gets funded mid-project, and academic stakeholders have authority over outcomes in ways that differ from a typical owner-contractor relationship. Managing change in that environment required a controls process that documented and absorbed scope evolution without destabilizing the baseline.

## Outcome

The project delivered a functional, adaptable research environment capable of supporting advanced neuroscience programs. My contribution was structural: keeping the controls framework intact through a high-change, high-complexity execution. Stakeholders stayed aligned, cost and schedule remained visible and defensible, and the transition to occupancy was orderly.

That kind of outcome doesn't happen by accident on a project this size. It requires someone consistently enforcing the controls discipline that makes decision-making possible — even when the project is moving fast and the pressure is to skip the process.
